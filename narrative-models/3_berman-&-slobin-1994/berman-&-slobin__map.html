There are many studies based on eliciting retellings of the Frog story (1979). Some of them analyze narrative macrostructure.
Most studies focus on microstructure, not macrostructure.
Findings from this type of study are limited, because it's a single story and not designed to control for complexity or cultural appropriateness like MAIN.
Frog story studies are often done on minority languages, and they represent a highly diverse set of languages/cultures.

Berman & Slobin_Coding schema

icon: LiCheckSmall

This is a reconstruction!

  • The coding schema is described in depth in Chapter I-B (pp.25-28).
  • I checked App II: Glossing & Transcription in case it might provide more details about coding schema; it doesn't but NB! p.660 definition of clause as unit of transcription.
  • The description is incomplete. More can be reconstructed by looking through analytical chapters (I started but then discontinued this task); see Berman & Slobin_Terms
  • Much of the coding is not deployed in the analysis (p.27), which makes it impossible to fully reconstruct.

Transcription is divided into clauses.

All clauses are labelled.
Some words (mostly verbs) are labelled when they fit certain categories.

Coding was not made through a standard method used in all languages.  
Rather, it was split into (1) analyzing forms = lg-specific, (2) analyzing functions = applicable across languages, hence lg-neutral.

expression labelled labelling what labelling how (1): category complexity labelling how (2): categories used
verb verb semantics lg-neutral function; nb unclear "change of state, physical state, internal state, causative activity, etc."
verb grammatical TAM lg-specific forms; nb varies; over 10 in total PRES, HAB, PRES.PROG, PRES.PERF, PAST.PERF, PFV, IPFV, PAST,... see p.115 for comparative + full view
multiple PoS (verbs and particles) lexical marking of aspect unclear
"expressions" (I guess AdP) lexical marking of sequential & other temporal relations lg-neutral function; nb unclear "after", "meanwhile"...; +"before"?, +etc?
clause type of clause lg-neutral function; 4 categories simple, complement, relative, adverbial subordinates
clause foreground (plot-advancing) VS background lg-neutral functions; 2 categories foreground, background

pp.85-106: categories from Trabasso & Nickels 1992 (-> Trabasso et al 1989, Stein & Glenn 1979) are used to model the stimulus story "Frog where are you", and for the coding schema for narrations of "Frog where are you" collected before B&S 1994

Berman & Slobin_Next

Their narrative model has broad applicability largely through vague, undefined terms. This avoids the problem of having a precise model that only fits certain languages/cultures. But it's very limited in terms of studying differences in macrostructure.
To be fair, this is also because their main focus is microstructure, not macrostructure.
