Short description

Stein & Glenn's model is the story grammar model from the 70-80s that gets cited the most. It's extremely granular. It focuses on modeling underlying stories, rather than narrations of stories. (It's the same distinction as deep syntax with nesting, empty categories,... vs surface-only.)

This confused me for a bit: the actual model used when citing Stein & Glenn is not theirs; it's a synthesis, most similar to Trabasso & Nickels 1992.

macro_X (1) Story "A story consists of a setting category plus an episode system" (p.59)
macro_X (2) Setting "The setting category (...) introduces the main character(s) and it describes the social, physical, or temporal context in which the remainder of the story occurs. (...) An activity of a character may also be included in the setting if it describes habitual behavior patterns." (p.59)
macro_X (1) Episode system "An episode system, like a story, is a higher order category and incorporates the entire story structure with the exception of the initial setting. An episode system consists of one or more episodes" (p.62); Episodes (and sets of episodes) are connected through 3 types of relationships, of which 2 are temporal (AND = simultaneous, THEN = sequence), and one causal (CAUSE).
macro_X (1) Episode "An episode is the primary higher order unit of a story and consists of an entire behavioral sequence (...) Inherent in this sequence is a causal chain of events beginning with an initiating event and ending with a resolution" (p.62); Initiating event + Response
macro_X (2) Initiating Event "The main function of an initiating event is to cause a response in the main character" (p.63); "An initiating event can be a natural occurrence, an action or an internal event (cognitive or emotional state)" (p.63)
(1) Response "The response consists of an internal response plus a plan sequence." (p.64)
macro_X (2) Internal Response "The internal response is an emotional state, a goal, and/or a cognitive state (knowing sth, remembering sth, believing sth,...)." (p.64)
(1) Plan Sequence "The plan sequence can be divided into the internal plan plus a plan application." (p.64)
(2) Internal Plan "The internal plan consists of a series of statements that define a character's strategy for obtaining a change in the situation" (p.64-65); "It is composed of two types of information: subgoals developed in order to achieve the main goal, and cognitions about the situation, the hypothesized activity and the consequences of the behavior." (p.65)
(1) Plan Application "The plan application refers to both a character's overt attempt to attain his goal and the resolution of his conflict or disequilibrium" (p.65)
macro_X (2) Attempt "The attempt includes statements referring to the character's overt actions to obtain a goal... The major function of attempt statements is to cause or lead to the resolution" (p.65)
macro_X (1) Resolution "the resolution (...) consists of the direct consequence and a reaction" (p.66)
(2) Direct Consequence "the three types of information included are: natural occurrences, actions, and end states" (p.66)
macro_X (1) Reaction "The type of statement [in the category Reaction] are affective responses, cognitions and action" (p.??)

Coding schema

Incomplete description (it's a single paragraph on p.88).
Data is given a score; no full transcription, no systematic data labelling.

Roughly corresponds to:

expression labelled labelling what labelling how (1): category complexity labelling how (2): categories used
N/A story grammar units over 20; exact nb depends on story length & complexity setting, initiating event, response,... (all concepts included in Episode could be categories)

Note, they creates story schemas for each original story (p.84-87), and used that as reference for the coding. This works well with storyboard retellings; it can also be useful for comparison between source material & non-storyboard retelling output.

Relevance today

Its focus on character cognitions had direct influence on several later models
Its high granularity & precision are note-worthy (but largely not adopted by later models)
