  • interactional linguistics
  • cross-cultural pragmatics; discourse studies (intersects with pragmatics); semantics
  • (?information structure, Frame)

The narrative structure models I've studied so far seem very disconnected from recent theory.
The model we adopt has a direct impact on how narrative structures are annotated (categories, but more importantly process).
I've started to read about pragmatics/semantics to pitch ideas for an updated model.

Looking at how these models coded their data does not seem very useful to us; it's incompletely described, and it's not an example of good practices.
I think a better alternative is to look into recent publications about best practices for linguistic annotation, like:

  • Handbook of Linguistic Annotation (general reference; book)
  • Hoek & Scholman 2017 (focuses on discourse; article)
    Best practices would impact the workflow, the labels used, and the description given in our writing.
    Learning about best practices can also be used to quality-check past annotations (& correct/re-do, or just learn from it for future projects).

From Nikitina 20XX:

  • Episode boundaries are sensitive to some types of expressions/functions; distribution is different at +start of ep VS -start of ep.
  • The forms populating the distribution are language-specific; so something like "nouns are overrepresented at the start of ep" is a falsified claim. That's true for some languages, and the opposite is true for others.
  • What's comparable across languages is: that there are similar distribution patterns (pure stats) + that there's a shared set of "types of expressions/functions sensitive to ep boundary" + that it organizes around a binary (+- start of ep).

From Ji 2002

Ji 2002 identifies these variables as salient indicators of episode boundaries. Meaning, when given the task to place episode boundaries, research participants highly agree in case of (+) conditions, and agree a lot less in case of (-) conditions.
The study was conducted in just one language (English), but it builds an inventory of functions & form types, rather than language-specific forms.

  • +- Marked sentence
  • +- Discontinuity in time, space
  • +- Discontinuity in theme, if it has an additional feature:
    • +- New participant
    • +- Anaphor
    • ==(non-participant theme with no callback = adds little-to-no episode boundary salience, for example.)
  • +- Major discontinuity (any type)

From House & Kadar 2021

  • linguaculture: "culture manifested through language use" (p.5)

    • linguacultures vary within and between languages; the point of "linguaculture" is to make a small claim, connected to a community of practice

  • speech acts:

    • typology of speech acts from Edmondson & House 1981 p.107
    • they warn against proliferation of new types of speech act; better to identify subtypes than new types

  • expression: "lexical items of various complexity" (p.73)

  • tertium comparationis: "A common quality in two entities to be compared, which is the basis of the comparison." (p.260); nb different types of stable variables possible

  • principle of comparability

  • standard situation:

    • "The notion of a standard situation involves participants’ rather fixed expectations and perceptions of social role. Role relations are transparent and predetermined, the requester has a right, the requestee an obligation, the degree of imposition involved in the request is low, as is the perceived degree of difficulty in realizing it. In a nutshell, the participants know where and who they are. Clearly, the distinction between a standard and a nonstandard situation is not clear cut." (p.76)
    • language use in standard situation can be studied in one linguaculture, then compared with another/several
    • they exclude study of language use in non-standard situations, and idiosyncratic uses

  • ritual VS conventionalization, ritual frame, RFIE (ritual frame-indicating expression)

    • ...

  • ancillary methodology vs comparative methodology:

    • ancillary methods are exploratory pilot studies
    • comparative methods (in cross-cultural pragmatics) = compare use of expressions in standard situation in at least 2 linguacultures

  • discourse

  • linking constructions (type of gambit): see p.144 + their source: https://benjamins.com/catalog/target.27.3.03hou
