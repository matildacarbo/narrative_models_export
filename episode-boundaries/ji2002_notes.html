p.1257: Episode transitions are defined by major changes in time, space, and/or theme.
When the content of a narrative lack such changes (absent, or change is not major), the episode boundary is less clear.

p.1259-1960. This study builds on:

  • Chafe (1979, 1980 = Pear story data), re types of discontinuity
  • Prideaux & Hogan (1993) + Givon (1993), re marked sentence structures

p.1262: Discontinuity tends to be introduced by marked structures. The study was conducted in English, so marked structure = sentence with any of these sentence-initially:

  • AdvP
  • PrepP
  • ParticipP
  • NounP
  • SubordinateP

Types of discontinuities labelled & quantified (p.1262-1263):

  • Temporal discontinuity
  • Spatial discontinuity
  • Thematic discontinuity (new theme or participant*. + NO explicit link with previous elements. //No data of this type in any group, see p. 1262 + 1266.//)
  • Thematic reorientation (type of thematic discontinuity = new theme or participant. + THERE IS explicit link with previous elements)
    (Thematic discontinuity vs reorientation = p.1265)
    * Participant means character in story.

Author divided data in three groups representing rates of agreement;
Group 1: >70% (p.1261)
Group 2: 40-69% (p.1265)
Group 3: 1-39% (p.1265)

(p.1261-1265) There is a high rate (> 70%) of agreement between research subjects, re: episode boundaries, when: there is a major change in time, place and/or theme, AND when there is a marked structure.
Regarding theme, agreement stays high when:

  • new theme is a participant (p.1264);
  • or there is an anaphor (callback to previous element; what they define as "thematic reorientation") (p.1265). //Looking further: Anaphora in group 1 all introduce "larger unit" or new participant + have a marked structure.//

(p.1266-1269) Rate of agreement re episode boundaries drops in these conditions.

  • (p.1266-1267) When there are less marked sentence structures (group 1 had 80% whereas group 2 had 41% and group 3 had 50%);
  • When thematic reorientation has either of these properties:
    • (p.1267) When new NON-PARTICIPANT theme is introduced, regardless of whether the sentence structure is marked. (Seen in group 2 & 3.)
    • (p.1268) When new theme introduction starts a small unit (group 3), agreement drop is larger than when it "initiates a larger unit" (group 2). //No further explanation included; examples supporting the statement are on p.1267+1268. I think this is about shifting between episodes 'on same level' vs shifting between those 'on different levels'.//
  • (p.1268) When the discontinuity (of any type; time-space-theme) is not major. (Author calls these "local".)

So, according to this study, the variables that make episode boundaries more or less salient are:

  • +- Marked sentence
  • +- Discontinuity in time, space
  • +- Discontinuity in theme, only with additional features:
    • +- New participant
    • +- Anaphor
    • (so non-participant theme with no callback = adds very little episode boundary salience, for example. NB Why not split Theme further & more precisely? + Anaphor is meaningful for the structure beyond this, at minimum in terms of NP vs Pron, what else?)
  • +- Major discontinuity (any type)

Source/claim mining

p.1258:
"It has also been established that narrative is not composed merely of sequences of sentences, but is hierarchically organized into intermediate units such as episodes or paragraphs (Bamberg and Marchman, 1991; Black and Bower, 1979; van Dijk, 1982; Grimes, 1975; Hinds, 1977, 1979; Longacre, 1979; Pu, Prideaux, and Stanford, 1992; Tomlin, 1987."

p.1259:
"Empirical support for the validity of episodes has been provided by psycholinguistic studies which indicate that people recalling stories treat information of an episode as an integral unit (cf. Black and Bower, 1979; Glenn, 1978; Mandler, 1978; Thorndyke, 1977), a result which is further supported by studies that have found the episode-shift effect, i.e., readers pause longer in processing episode-initial sentences that introduce an episode shift than they do for episode-internal sentences (Haberlandt, Berian, and Sandson, 1980; Mandler and Goodman, 1982)"

p.1264:
"Many researchers have proposed that the default assumption of readers is that the order in which the events are reported corresponds to the chronological order of these events (e.g., Comrie, 1985; Dowty, 1986; Fleischman, 1990; Givon, 1992; Hopper, 1979). This has been called the iconicity assumption (Fleischman, 1990; Hopper, 1979)."
